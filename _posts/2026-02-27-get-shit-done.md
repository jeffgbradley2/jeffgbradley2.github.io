How many hours per workday do you spend on bullshit?

If you're reading this, then you're probably a tech worker of some kind. I bet you spend _hours_ every day in meetings, reading and sending email, slack messages, explaining things, giving status updates. Maybe not, but even if it isn't hours, or isn't those specific things, I'm willing to bet that there is a large portion of your time wasted on pseudo-work.

According to studies done by Asana and Microsoft, knowledge workers spend _less than half_ of their working hours on skilled work. The rest is coordination overhead: interruptions, unproductive meetings, managing email. Meetings about meetings. Emails about emails. The Slack and Salesforce Workforce Lab found that employees spend _a third of their day_ on low-value work. Thus, much of what knowledge workers do all day isn't real work.

On the other hand, any large system requires governance, and entails bureaucracy. Bureaucracy, by its nature, entails a bunch of pseudo-work. It's a necessary evil, all the meetings and reports, the assessments and checklists. Necessary, but oh so costly, and wasteful.

I have a theory: by automating bureaucratic processes and reducing pseudo-work, companies can capitalize on the benefits of those processes, while focusing on those activities that move the needle forward. If we automate the BS, we can get back to focusing on adding real value.

I've experienced a lot of pseudo-work in my career. There are entire roles within organizations I have seen for whom _everything_ is pseudo-work, wasting not only company time, but the time of other people as well. I routinely have project managers, for example, that schedule meetings with me so that I can tell them what is going on, so that they can create a power point presentation to give to someone else, to tell them what is going on. And that is just the tip of the iceberg.

Pseudo-work was introduced by Normark and Jensen in their book titled (surprise) Pseudo-Work. I'm familiar with the concept through Cal Newport's work. Essentially, there are "bullshit jobs" in knowledge work, jobs that simply check boxes, creating the appearance of useful activity through meetings and paperwork and reporting. Newport coined the term "pseudo-productivity" for this proxy of real productive work.

But busyness does not equal productivity, just like attending a meeting does not equate to contribution. It's all theater, a visible dance, with everyone so busy getting nothing done. I have literally been in standups where individuals report, week after week, that they are "so busy, supporting everyone, lots of meetings, just so much going on." I'm serious!

And it is self-perpetuating. The more an organization allows pseudo-work, the more it seeps into everyone's workdays. The hard working team members, heads down solving real problems, get pulled into meetings, or distracted with slack messages requesting that they review a power point or status update. I have people routinely ask me to review power points they created about projects I am working on. This requires me to waste my time reading content they generated, correcting it because they don't know what is going on, and: Poof! An hour of _my_ time is gone!

But the times, they are a changing. AI systems that I work with daily are capable of automating most, if not all, of this pseudo work out of existence. Studies from multiple sources (Stanford, MIT, IBM) show _massive_ productivity gains from introducing AI into processes to automate the busy work. These tools can, for instance, automatically transcribe meetings (no more furious typing by the PM while everyone else talks), convert those into minutes (no more hours spent manually typing that out), and automatically create tickets for action items or even update plans or specs and submit MR.

Let's be honest: most meetings should be an email. But we can do better than that now. Give an AI agent access to Jira, Confluence, your repo, and the status update writes itself. The meeting disappears. The email disappears. The PowerPoint that nobody wanted to make and nobody wanted to read disappears. All of it: automated away.

I've experienced this firsthand. When I started treating AI not as a chatbot I occasionally ask questions, but as an embedded part of how I work, drafting documents, pulling data from multiple systems, generating reports that used to take hours, something shifted. I went from spending half my day on overhead to spending most of it on the actual engineering problems I was hired to solve.

This is what "AI-First" actually means. Not AI as an add-on you bolt onto existing processes. A complete redesign of how work flows through a team, with automation handling the coordination layer so that humans focus on judgment, architecture, and the problems that require expertise. Microsoft calls this the "Frontier Firm" and describes it in phases: AI as assistant, AI as digital colleague, AI as process operator. Most of us are still in phase one, asking ChatGPT questions in a browser tab. The real gains come when you move past that, when AI is woven into the workflow itself. That's when the pseudo-work starts to melt away.

The whole point of automating pseudo-work is to create more space for the human interactions that actually matter. Collaboration, debate, mentorship, the things that build culture and push ideas forward. I'm not advocating for removing that. What I _am_ advocating is that we remove is the need for "the coordinator" or "the typer", the person who sets up the meeting and asks people what they are doing. Automate the creation of artifacts, the bureaucratic process of reporting and documenting everything, so that team members can focus on the problems they are solving and truly collaborate. And so they can stop wasting each other's time!

Expert judgement, creativity, relationships, strategic thinking, and mastery of our domains is ours. It is what adds value to the organization. The experts are the ones that get shit done, in a very human way, augmented by AI. The rest is pseudo-work, and that we can and should automate out of existence.

My experience with ISO 42001 and ISO 9001 is an interesting case study. Those frameworks, and the management systems that implement them, _require_ a certain amount of pseudo-work. They are large management systems and by their nature bureaucratic. In many organizations, such systems are a major drag on speed and productivity.

But it doesn't have to be this way, not anymore. Now, with automation and discipline, these processes can achieve the holy grail of maximizing performance while minimizing friction. Automated policy enforcement; AI risk assessment (with human review); continuous audits, programmatic enforcement of compliance. Those huge performance reports that took days to generate? They can be done in seconds now. The week of data analysis across multiple source systems? An agent can do that while you go to the bathroom.

IBM found that 27% of AI efficiency gains come directly from strong governance. Not in spite of it, but because of it. Governance, done right, is what lets you reap the benefits traditionally reserved for massive companies with huge compliance departments, while moving far faster, at a fraction of the cost. But you have to stay disciplined. Automate the bureaucratic process, don't replace it with review pseudo-work that recreates the same drag in a new form. Trust the experts to do their jobs.

Many companies are embracing this shift. Shopify reported in 2023 that they cancelled all recurring meetings with more than 3 people, resulting in a saving of over 76,000 hours and 25% increase in completed projects. A study by the MIT Sloan Management Review found that companies implementing no-meetings days reduced total meetings by 40%, with measurable gains in autonomy, communication, and productivity. And more recently, multiple studies out of Stanford, Harvard, and MIT reveal significant productivity increases from adopting AI automation.

Now is the time to start rethinking roles and processes. It isn't easy, and for some organizations, it may be impossible. I've heard tech CEOs say bluntly that most organizations would have to completely redesign their structure, and simply aren't capable of doing that. But for those of us who can, who see the value and _want_ to improve, to shed the burden of pseudo-work and embrace an AI-first approach, there has never been a better time. The tools exist today, and are incredibly affordable. The organizations and teams that embrace them with the proper governance will thrive in this new and changing environment, while the legacy ones clinging to pseudo-work decline.

So stop treating busyness as productivity. The goal of work isn't to fill up a calendar with meetings or to give status reports. The goal is to get shit done, real work that moves the business toward its objectives. Real experts, motivated, empowered by automation, governed by systems that trust them, as they _actually build things_.
